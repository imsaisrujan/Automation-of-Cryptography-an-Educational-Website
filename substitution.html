Substitution Techniques

Substitution technique is a classical encryption approach where the characters present in the initial message are restored by the other characters or numbers or by symbols. If the plain text (original message) is treated as the string of bits, thus the substitution technique would restore bit pattern of plain text with the bit pattern of cipher text.

Types of Substitution Techniques

MonoAlphabetic Cipher

Monoalphabetic cipher is a type of encryption technique in cryptography where each character of the plain text is mapped to another fixed character of the cipher text.

Caesar Cipher

The Caesar cipher is one of the simplest and most widely known cryptography techniques. It is a type of substitution cipher in which each letter in the plaintext is shifted a certain number of places down or up the alphabet.

PolyAlphabetic Cipher

The Polyalphabetic cipher is a substitution cipher in which the plaintext letters are encrypted using multiple alphabets. This provides a higher level of security compared to simpler ciphers like the Caesar cipher..

PlayFair Cipher

The Playfair Cipher is a manual symmetric encryption technique and was the first literal digraph substitution cipher. It was invented in 1854 by Charles Wheatstone but was named after Lord Playfair who promoted its use. It was used for tactical purposes by British forces in the Second Boer War and the First World War and was also used by the Australian Army during World War II.

Hill Cipher

The Hill Cipher is a polygraphic substitution cipher based on linear algebra. It was invented by Lester S. Hill in 1929 and is one of the first known examples of encryption to use a matrix.
